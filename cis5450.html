Predicting 30-Day Readmission in Diabetic Patients

An end-to-end big-data ML pipeline that flags diabetic patients at high risk of 30-day hospital readmission — so care teams can target transitional-care resources and avoid CMS penalties.

Project Overview

  • ~100k US diabetic hospital encounters; tables joined and wrangled with DuckDB and Polars
  • Patient-level split (no leakage), SMOTE in-pipeline, and RandomizedSearch tuning on ROC AUC
  • Team project with Panagiotis Giadikiaroglou, Connor Wang, and Panagiotis Galanis

Model comparison — XGBoost and Logistic Regression tie at ROC AUC ≈ 0.62, both beating the 11.3% base rate on precision–recall.

Why It Matters & the Problem

Under the CMS Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program (HRRP), hospitals face direct financial penalties for excess 30-day readmissions. Identifying high-risk diabetic patients before they are discharged is both a clinical and an operational priority: a model that flags them at discharge lets hospitals concentrate transitional-care resources — medication reconciliation, discharge education — where they have the highest expected impact.

We framed it as a binary classification problem (readmitted within 30 days = 1, otherwise 0) over ~100k US diabetic hospital encounters. With only an 11.3% positive class, accuracy is a misleading metric, so we optimized and evaluated on ROC AUC and F1 / average precision instead.

Data Engineering & Feature Engineering

  • Joined demographics, diagnoses, medications, lab results, and prior-utilization tables via DuckDB; fast group-by risk profiling with Polars
  • Collapsed ~unique ICD-9 diagnosis codes into broad clinical categories with regex matching on the official ICD-CM chapter ranges
  • Engineered total_prior_visits (inpatient + outpatient + emergency) and num_drug_changes
  • Added a KMeans acuity-cluster label (four patient profiles via the elbow method) that surfaced in the model's top features

Modeling & Results

The pipeline was built to be honest about leakage and imbalance: a GroupShuffleSplit on patient_nbr keeps any single patient entirely in train or test (many patients have multiple encounters), and SMOTE runs inside an imbalanced-learn pipeline so synthetic samples only ever touch the training folds. RandomizedSearchCV tuned each model on ROC AUC, comparing Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost.

XGBoost edged out Logistic Regression for the best ROC AUC (~0.62 vs Random Forest's 0.59), and both beat the base-rate baseline on precision–recall. Because the default 0.5 threshold yields near-zero recall under this imbalance, we calibrated the decision threshold down to ~0.14 (best-F1) to prioritize catching true readmissions — the clinically correct trade-off, since missing a high-risk patient is far costlier than an extra follow-up call.

Three statistical hypotheses were all rejected (p < 0.05): HbA1c testing, emergency admission, and a higher medication count each associate with elevated readmission risk. The dominant predictor overall was prior utilization (number_inpatient and the engineered total_prior_visits) — outweighing any single lab value.

Actionable Insight

A Polars group-by over discharge disposition turned the analysis into a roadmap: patients transferred to Skilled Nursing Facilities form the largest high-risk group, making post-discharge destination a primary, targetable predictor of readmission risk.
